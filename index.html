Social Class Shapes our Online and Offline Trajectories

The social classes of people have important impact on our behaviors in both online and offline space. In this research, we focus on understanding how social class influences our use of digital media and the solidification of social stratification by digital media. Using a large-scale smartphone dataset of 100,000 users in a major city of China, we constructed two weighted networks to trace how users move in the virtual world (attention network) and in the physical world (mobility network). The findings indicate that the people of higher social class is strongly linked to larger mobility ranges and more extensive reading contents compared with the people of lower social class. Additionally, the significant difference in the patterns of schedules, lifestyles, social needs, and access to locations supplies more information for understanding the gap between the rich and the poor, and it also tends to suggest the solidification of social class.

June 15, 2019

The Cinderella Complex

Gender norms and stereotypes are deeply rooted in society and are reinforced through the recreation of cultural products. Our analysis of thousands of novels, movie synopsis, and movie scripts reveals how these culture products promote gender stereotypes in a quiet but strong way. We start from demonstrating how the happiness of Cinderella depends on the Prince but not vice versa. This analysis, based on the word embedding technique, provides a novel approach to identify how gender stereotypes in our case, men are women’s way to happiness - are created in stories through a carefully designed narrative structure. This structure of narrative builds up and enhances “Cinderella complex”, women's fear of independence and unconscious desire to be taken care of by others. The used computational techniques allows us to scale up analysis to include 1,327 novels, 6,657 movie synopsis, and 1,109 movie scripts, which covers a substantial portion of collective narratives as both the consequence and the cause of how society thinks. The observed widely existence of “Cinderella complex” across periods and cultures reminds the necessary to combat against it through the creation of new narrative in education, policy, and beyond.

Manuscript: https://arxiv.org/abs/1811.04599
